The goal of the course is to present those notions of number theory and abstract algebra which are necessary for the understanding of the modern applications of those branches of mathematics in computer science, e.g. in cryptography.

  1. Groups, rings, fields. Operations on complex numbers.
  2. Divisibility. Greatest common divisor. Euclidean algorithm and Bezout's theorem.
  3. Modular arithmetic, congruences. Linear Diophantine equations. Prime numbers.
  4. Matrices, operations in the set of matrices with coefficients in a field.
  5. Systems of linear equations. Gauss-Jordan elimination.
  6. Determinants.
  7. Finding inverse matrix.
  8. Cramer's rule.

The course is a basic introduction to the most important computer science ideas and concepts. Topics vary from short historical background of computer science, through data representation, logic circuit design to programming languages, operating system concepts and computer networks. During the tutorials students acquire skills in using different numeral systems, boolean formula transformation, different data (e.g. numbers or images) representation methods. The emphasis is placed on learning how to describe algorithms and how to use them to solve given problem. A sepecial attention is paid to adapt knowledge for a specific problem (student has to justify his/her choice, give appropriate example etc.)

  1. Numeral systems with an emphasis on base 2, 8, 10 and 16 number system - conversion, arithmetical operations.
  2. Bitwise operations.
  3. Methods for data representation with examples for characters (ASCII, Unicode), numbers (integers and floating points), graphic file, TCP/IP stack protocol.
  4. Boolean algebra - definitions, theorems; transformation of boolean formulas (Karnaugh maps).
  5. Introduction to logic circuits design.
  6. TuringMachine and introduction to architecture of modern computers.
  7. Introduction to formal languages; BNF notation; regular expressions.
  8. Notion of algorithm. Methods of description of algorithm - flowchart, pseudocode, natural language.
  9. Programming languages - types and classifications.
  10. Operating systems - main taskas, types and examples.
  11. Computer networks - main concepts about architecture, topology and hardware.
The aim of the course is to familiarize the students with the basics concepts of programming in high-level programming languages (using a one chosen). The topics covered by this course are basic notions and structures used in programming (variables, data types, statements, input/output procedures, subprograms), as well as prepararion of programs applying the mentioned elements (together with developing algorithms to solve the problems) and analysing their corrrectness.

  1. Structure and components of a C++ program. Preprocessor directives. Header files.
  2. Input and Output expressions.
  3. Variables and basic built-in types.
  4. Operators and expressions.
  5. Control structures. Loops and decisions.
  6. Compound built-in types: references, pointers, arrays.
  7. Functions definition, declaration and execution.
  8. Methods of argument passing to a function. Value returning from a function.
  9. Overloaded functions.
  10. Default values for formal arguments in functions.
  11. Dynamic memory allocation.
  12. Recursion and its implementations.

Legal aspect of computer science

The course is aimed at students who are interested in the legal aspects of computer science. The course is divided into two parts:
  1. The first part covers the legal aspects of computer science,
  2. The second part covers the legal aspects of computer science in the context of the field of information technology.
The main aim of the course is to familiarize students with various basic information technology.
